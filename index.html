How the Hilliness Score Works

The hilliness score combines three dimensions of what makes a route feel hilly. Each component uses logarithmic scaling, which expands differences between gentle and moderate routes while compressing the extreme end — matching how runners perceive difficulty.

1. Climb Density (Volume) — 20% weight

Total elevation gained divided by total distance. Log-scaled with a ceiling of ~160 ft/mi.

score = log(1 + gain_per_km) / log(1 + 30) × 100

2. Gradient-Weighted Intensity — 35% weight

For each climbing segment, the gradient is raised to the power of 2.0 and multiplied by segment distance. The squared exponent means a 12% grade contributes 4× more than a 6% grade (not just 2×). Log-scaled with a ceiling of 20.

raw = Σ(distance_i × gradient_i²) / total_distance  →  score = log(1 + raw) / log(1 + 20) × 100

3. Steep Proportion (Character) — 45% weight

The fraction of climbing distance above 8% gradient. This component carries the most weight because it's the biggest perceptual differentiator — routes with similar total gain feel very different depending on how that gain is distributed. Log-scaled with a ceiling at 50%.

steep_ratio = distance_above_8% / total_climb_distance  →  log-scaled 0–100

Why Logarithmic Scaling?

Linear scaling compresses gentle-to-moderate routes into a narrow band at the bottom. Log scaling acts like a magnifying glass on the lower end: the difference between 50 and 70 ft/mi gain gets amplified, while the difference between 200 and 220 ft/mi gets compressed. This matches how runners perceive effort — the jump from flat to rolling feels much bigger than the jump from hilly to very hilly.

Pre-processing

GPS elevation data goes through three filtering passes. First, a median filter (window size 7) removes sharp elevation spikes caused by GPS drift during watch pauses. Second, a moving average (window size 5) smooths remaining noise. Third, a 3-meter dead-band threshold eliminates GPS wobble — small oscillations under 3m (~10ft) that aren't real terrain changes but accumulate into significant phantom elevation gain. This approach mirrors Strava's elevation correction and produces gain figures consistent with corrected industry values. Sub-1.5ft segments are discarded as GPS jitter. Only uphill segments (>0.5% grade) contribute to the score.
